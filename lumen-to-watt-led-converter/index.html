Key Terms Explained
Lumen (lm)
The standard unit of visible light output. More lumens means a brighter bulb. A typical 60W incandescent produces about 800 lumens.
Watt (W)
A unit of electrical power consumption. It tells you how much electricity a bulb uses, not how much light it produces.
Luminous Efficacy
The ratio of lumens produced per watt consumed (lm/W). Higher efficacy = more light for less electricity. LEDs reach 80-120 lm/W vs. 14 lm/W for incandescents.
Color Temperature (Kelvin)
Measured in Kelvin (K), this describes the warmth or coolness of a light's color. 2700K is warm yellow; 5000K+ is cool blue-white daylight.
Color Rendering Index (CRI)
A scale from 0 to 100 rating how accurately a light source renders colors compared to natural sunlight. A CRI of 90+ is excellent for kitchens and art studios.
Incandescent Bulb
The classic bulb invented by Edison. A tungsten filament glows when heated by electricity. About 90% of its energy is wasted as heat, not light.
Light Emitting Diode (LED)
A semiconductor device that produces light when current flows through it. LEDs are 6 to 7 times more efficient than incandescents and last 15 to 25 times longer.
CFL (Compact Fluorescent)
A coiled fluorescent tube designed to replace incandescents. About 4 times more efficient than incandescent, but contains mercury and takes time to reach full brightness.

The Complete Guide to LED Bulb Replacement and Lighting Efficiency

Switching from incandescent or halogen bulbs to LEDs is one of the fastest-payback home upgrades you can make. But with watts, lumens, Kelvin ratings, and CRI numbers all on the box, buying the right bulb can feel surprisingly complicated. This guide explains the science, walks you through how to use this calculator, and answers the questions most homeowners have before making the switch.

Why Watts Stopped Being Useful

For most of the 20th century, buying a light bulb was simple: higher watts meant more light. That worked because all incandescent bulbs converted roughly the same proportion of electricity into visible light - about 10 to 15 lumens per watt. When CFLs arrived, the watt-to-brightness relationship broke down. A 13W CFL produces the same 800 lumens as a 60W incandescent. LEDs broke it further: an 8 to 10W LED now matches that same 800 lumens.

The U.S. Federal Trade Commission updated light bulb labeling requirements in 2011 to require lumens on the front of the package and watts as secondary information. The EU followed suit. Today, lumens are the correct way to shop for light, and watts only tell you what your electricity bill will look like.

The Math Behind the Results

This calculator uses standard industry luminous efficacy values to convert between watts and lumens for legacy bulb types. Incandescent bulbs produce approximately 14 lumens per watt. Halogen bulbs, which are an improved form of incandescent, produce approximately 18 lumens per watt. CFLs produce approximately 60 lumens per watt. To find the equivalent lumens for your old bulb, the tool multiplies its wattage by the appropriate efficacy rate.

For the LED equivalent, the calculator divides the target lumens by 90 lm/W and rounds to the nearest whole watt. Modern standard LEDs produce approximately 80 to 100 lumens per watt, with 90 lm/W being a solid real-world average across commonly available A19, BR30, and PAR bulbs. Premium LED brands may reach 110 to 130 lm/W, meaning the actual bulb you buy may be slightly lower wattage than the calculator suggests - the result shown is a conservative estimate.

Understanding the Kelvin Scale

Color temperature is one of the most important - and most misunderstood - attributes of an LED bulb. The Kelvin scale runs from warm amber at around 1800K up to crisp blue-white at 6500K and beyond. Choosing the wrong color temperature is the most common reason people are disappointed with LED replacements.

For living rooms, bedrooms, and dining areas where you want a warm, relaxing atmosphere similar to old incandescent bulbs, choose 2700K (Soft White) or 3000K (Warm White). For kitchens, bathrooms, offices, and workbenches where you want crisp, clear visibility, choose 3500K to 4100K (Bright White or Cool White). For garages, studios, workshops, and reading tasks where maximum visual acuity matters, choose 5000K to 6500K (Daylight). The visual reference bar in this tool shows the progression from warm to cool at a glance.
