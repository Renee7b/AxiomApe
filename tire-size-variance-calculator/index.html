Key Terms Explained
Aspect Ratio
The sidewall height expressed as a percentage of the section width. A 50-series tire on a 225mm-wide tire has a sidewall that is 112.5mm tall (50% of 225).
Section Width
The total width of an inflated tire from one sidewall to the other, measured in millimeters. The first number in a metric tire size (e.g., 225 in 225/50R17).
Wheel Diameter
The diameter of the rim in inches. This is the last number in a tire size (e.g., 17 in 225/50R17) and must match the rim you are mounting the tire on.
Revolutions per Mile
How many complete tire rotations it takes to travel one mile. Computed as 63,360 (inches per mile) divided by the tire's circumference in inches. Your speedometer uses this figure internally.
Speedometer Calibration
The factory programming that converts wheel rotation speed into a displayed road speed. It is fixed to your OEM tire's revolutions per mile, which is why any diameter change shifts the reading.
Plus-Sizing
Moving to a larger rim diameter while reducing the tire aspect ratio to maintain a near-identical overall rolling diameter. A +1 fitment uses a rim one inch larger than stock.
Unsprung Weight
The mass of components not supported by the suspension springs, including the wheel, tire, brake rotor, and caliper. Heavier wheels increase unsprung weight and can degrade ride quality and handling.
AWD Tire Tolerance
The maximum allowable diameter difference between tires on an all-wheel drive vehicle before drivetrain damage risk increases significantly. Most manufacturers specify 3% or less, and many require all four tires to match.

The Complete Guide to Tire Size Comparisons and Speedometer Calibration

Swapping wheels is one of the most common and impactful upgrades a driver can make. But changing the tire size without understanding the dimensional consequences can lead to an inaccurate speedometer, voided warranties, ABS malfunctions, and in AWD vehicles, expensive drivetrain damage. This guide covers everything you need to make an informed fitment decision.

How the Tire Math Works

A metric tire size carries all the geometry you need in three numbers. The section width in millimeters multiplied by the aspect ratio percentage gives you the sidewall height in millimeters. Divide by 25.4 to convert to inches. Double it (two sidewalls) and add the wheel diameter to get the total rolling diameter. Multiply by pi to get circumference. Divide 63,360 by the circumference to get revolutions per mile. The ratio of the two circumferences tells you exactly how much the speedometer will be off.

For example: a 225/50R17 has a sidewall of 112.5mm, or 4.43 inches. Total diameter is 25.86 inches. Circumference is 81.23 inches. Revolutions per mile is 780. Swapping to a 245/45R18 raises the diameter to 26.69 inches, a 3.2% increase, which means at 60 MPH displayed you are traveling about 62 MPH.

Speedometer Error: Why It Matters

A speedometer reading that is 3% low means every time you think you are driving 60 MPH you are actually doing 61.8. That is a meaningful margin in school zones, work zones, and on highways with automated enforcement. Odometer error compounds over time as well: if the odometer reads low, your oil change intervals stretch beyond their safe limit and your actual mileage is understated for resale purposes.

Plus-Sizing and Keeping Your Rolling Diameter Matched

The goal of a well-executed plus-size fitment is to land within 1-2% of the OEM rolling diameter while gaining the visual and handling benefits of a larger rim. Wider, lower-profile tires have a larger contact patch, shorter sidewalls for crisper steering response, and room for bigger brake rotors. The key is balancing the wider width against a lower aspect ratio so the circumference stays close to stock. This calculator lets you experiment with different sizes until you find the combination that minimizes speedometer error.

Frequently Asked Questions

A metric tire size like 225/50R17 breaks down into three numbers. The first number (225) is the section width in millimeters, measured from sidewall to sidewall. The second number (50) is the aspect ratio: the sidewall height expressed as a percentage of the section width. So a 50 aspect ratio means the sidewall is 50% of 225mm, or 112.5mm tall. The letter R stands for radial construction. The last number (17) is the wheel diameter in inches that the tire is designed to mount on.
Your speedometer counts wheel rotations per unit of time and converts that rotation rate to a road speed using a fixed value: the circumference of your OEM tire. When you install tires with a different circumference, the rotation rate stays the same for any given road speed but the distance covered per rotation changes. A larger circumference means the car travels farther per rotation, so the actual road speed is higher than displayed. A smaller circumference has the opposite effect. The percentage difference in circumference equals the percentage speedometer error.
Plus-sizing means moving to a larger wheel diameter while reducing the tire aspect ratio to compensate, keeping the overall rolling diameter as close to stock as possible. A +1 fitment uses a rim one inch larger than OEM (for example, 17 to 18 inches). The aspect ratio drops to prevent the total diameter from growing too much. Plus-sizing allows wider, lower-profile tires that offer a larger contact patch, sharper handling, and clearance for bigger brake rotors, all while keeping the speedometer error minimal. A well-executed plus-size should land within 1-2% of the OEM diameter.
AWD and 4WD drivetrains expect all four tires to rotate at nearly identical speeds for a given road speed. The center differential and transfer case are engineered around this assumption. If one pair of tires has a meaningfully different diameter, those tires complete a different number of revolutions per mile. The drivetrain interprets this as constant wheel slip and keeps the AWD system partially engaged even on dry pavement. This causes the transfer case to overheat, wears differentials prematurely, and in severe cases causes catastrophic failure. Most AWD manufacturers specify a maximum allowable mismatch of 1/4 inch in total diameter between all four tires.
The widely accepted automotive standard is 3% or less variance in overall diameter from OEM. Beyond that threshold, ABS wheel speed sensors may register false differences between axles, causing unexpected ABS activation or disabling stability control. AWD systems are at increased risk of drivetrain damage. For plus-sizing specifically, most fitment guides target staying within 1-2% to preserve speedometer accuracy and suspension geometry. This calculator displays a red safety warning badge any time the variance you enter exceeds the 3% threshold.
