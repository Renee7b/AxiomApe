Key Terms Explained
Intermittent Fasting
An eating pattern that cycles between defined periods of fasting and eating. It does not prescribe specific foods; it focuses on when you eat.
Autophagy
The body's cellular recycling process. Damaged proteins and organelles are broken down and reused. It ramps up noticeably after roughly 16 to 18 hours of fasting.
Insulin Sensitivity
How effectively your cells respond to insulin. Fasting periods lower baseline insulin levels, which over time improves the body's sensitivity and reduces metabolic disease risk.
Ketosis
A metabolic state in which the body burns fat for fuel by producing ketone bodies, typically entered after 12 to 16 hours of fasting once liver glycogen is depleted.
OMAD
One Meal A Day. A 23:1 intermittent fasting protocol where all daily calories are consumed in a single one-hour eating window. Considered an advanced approach.
16:8 Protocol
Fast for 16 consecutive hours, eat within an 8-hour window. The most widely studied and beginner-friendly form of time-restricted eating.
Circadian Rhythm
The 24-hour internal clock governing sleep, hormone release, and digestion. The 13:11 Circadian fasting protocol aligns eating with daylight hours to support natural metabolic cycles.
Glycogen Depletion
The process of exhausting stored glucose (glycogen) in the liver and muscles. Once glycogen runs low, the body shifts to burning fatty acids and producing ketones for energy.

Choosing the Right Protocol for Your Goals

The 16:8 protocol (also called Lean Gains) is the starting point for most people. You skip breakfast, eat your first meal around noon, and close your eating window by 8 PM. The 16-hour fast is long enough for glycogen depletion and the early stages of autophagy, but short enough to maintain social flexibility.

The 18:6 and 20:4 protocols are more aggressive versions suited to people who have already adapted to 16:8 and want deeper fat oxidation or more pronounced autophagy. The 20:4 Warrior Diet compresses eating into a 4-hour late-afternoon or evening window, which aligns with some research on circadian hormone patterns.

OMAD (23:1) is the most restrictive common protocol. All daily nutrition comes from a single meal. It demands careful attention to nutrient density to avoid deficiencies. Not recommended as a starting point.

The 13:11 Circadian Rhythm protocol is the gentlest option. It asks you to eat only during daylight hours, aligning food intake with your body's natural insulin sensitivity peaks in the morning and early afternoon. It is an excellent entry point for those new to fasting or who have difficulty with longer windows.

Time Math: How Midnight Crossover Works

Many common fasting schedules cross midnight. For example, a 16:8 schedule anchored to a noon first meal means the fasting window runs from 8:00 PM to 12:00 PM the next day. This calculator handles all midnight crossovers automatically using 24-hour math, so you will never see negative hour values or NaN errors regardless of your anchor time.

Metabolic Milestones During a Fast

Hour 4 to 8: Blood glucose and insulin return to baseline after the last meal. Hour 12: Liver glycogen is largely depleted; the body begins shifting toward fat oxidation. Hour 13 to 16: Ketosis begins in most people. Hour 16 to 18: Autophagy activity begins to ramp up measurably. Hour 24 and beyond: Deeper autophagy states, significant growth hormone elevation, and more pronounced anti-inflammatory effects. These milestones are approximate and vary by individual metabolic rate, activity level, and prior meal composition.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best intermittent fasting schedule for beginners? +
The 16:8 protocol is the most beginner-friendly intermittent fasting schedule. You fast for 16 hours and eat during an 8-hour window. A common starting point is skipping breakfast and eating from noon to 8 PM. It fits naturally around a normal sleep schedule, so 6 to 8 of those fasting hours happen while you sleep.
Does drinking black coffee or water break my fast? +
Plain water, black coffee (no milk, cream, or sweeteners), and plain unsweetened tea do not break a metabolic fast. They contain negligible calories and do not trigger an insulin response. Anything with calories, including bulletproof coffee with butter or MCT oil, technically breaks the fast from a strict caloric standpoint, though opinions differ on whether it disrupts autophagy.
What is autophagy and how many hours of fasting does it take? +
Autophagy is the cellular self-cleaning process where your body identifies and recycles damaged proteins and organelles. Research suggests autophagy activity begins to ramp up noticeably after roughly 16 to 18 hours of fasting, though the exact threshold varies by individual, activity level, and diet. Some studies indicate deeper autophagy at 24 to 72 hours, which is the range targeted by extended fasting protocols.
Can I change my fasting and eating windows on the weekends? +
Yes. Many people use a relaxed weekend protocol, for example switching from 16:8 on weekdays to 14:10 on Saturday and Sunday. This tool includes a Weekend Relaxed Schedule toggle that automatically adjusts Saturday and Sunday to a shorter fasting window while keeping your weekday protocol intact.
How do I calculate a 16:8 fasting schedule if I work night shifts? +
Use the anchor time input and set it to your first meal time after waking. For example, if you finish a night shift at 7 AM and eat your first meal at 8 AM, set the anchor to 8:00 AM and choose 16:8. The calculator will set your eating window from 8 AM to 4 PM, with fasting from 4 PM until 8 AM the next day. The tool handles midnight crossover automatically.
