Key Terms Explained
Cubic Yard
The standard unit for ordering and pricing concrete. One cubic yard equals 27 cubic feet (a cube 3 ft on each side). Ready-mix trucks and concrete prices are quoted per cubic yard.
Portland Cement
The binding ingredient in concrete: a fine gray powder made by heating limestone and clay in a kiln. When mixed with water it forms a paste that hardens and holds aggregate particles together.
Aggregate
The bulk filler in concrete. Coarse aggregate (gravel or crushed stone) provides compressive strength and volume; fine aggregate (sand) fills the voids between stones and improves workability.
Slump
A field test measuring concrete workability. A fresh sample is placed in a cone mold, the mold is removed, and the distance the mix sags (slumps) is measured. Higher slump = wetter, more workable mix.
Screeding
Dragging a straight board (screed) across freshly poured concrete to level it flush with the form edges. Screeding is done immediately after placement, before the mix begins to stiffen.
Footing
A concrete pad or pier placed at or below grade to distribute load into the soil. Footings prevent settling and must extend below the frost line in cold climates.
Waste Factor
An added percentage (typically 5 to 15%) above the calculated volume to cover spillage, uneven sub-grades, form leakage, and measurement rounding. Running out mid-pour creates a structural cold joint.
Curing
The chemical hydration process by which concrete gains strength over time. Proper curing requires sustained moisture for at least 7 days. Concrete reaches about 70% of its 28-day strength in the first week.

The Complete Guide to Estimating Concrete for Slabs, Footings, and Columns

Getting your concrete volume right before a pour is the single most important step in any concrete project. Order too little and you face a cold joint: a structural weakness where the first load begins to set before the second batch arrives. Order too much and you pay for material you cannot use and must dispose of. This guide walks through the math behind each calculation, explains why the waste factor matters, and helps you decide between bag concrete and a ready-mix truck.

How to Use This Calculator

Select your pour type using the tabs at the top of the card. Use "Slab / Square Pad" for patios, driveways, and flat pads. Use "Round Footing / Column" for Sonotube tube piers and circular footings. Use "Square Column / Pier" for rectangular post bases and block piers.

Enter your dimensions using the paired fields. Each dimension has two fields: a major unit (feet or meters) and a minor unit (inches or centimeters). This lets you enter mixed measurements naturally, for example a 10 ft 6 in slab length without converting anything. A 4-inch slab depth can be entered as 0 ft and 4 in. The calculator handles the unit conversion automatically.

Move the Waste Factor slider to match your project. A well-formed flat slab on compacted gravel can often use 5 to 8 percent. Hand-excavated round footings or any pour over a rough sub-grade should use 10 to 15 percent. All outputs update in real time as you adjust the slider.

Understanding the Calculation

Every result starts by computing the net volume in cubic feet. For slabs and square piers: Volume = Length x Width x Depth. For round footings and columns: Volume = Pi x (Diameter / 2) x (Diameter / 2) x Depth x Quantity. The waste factor is then applied as a multiplier: a net volume of 18 cubic feet at 10% waste becomes 19.8 cubic feet ordered. Dividing by 27 converts to cubic yards (since 1 cubic yard = 3 ft x 3 ft x 3 ft = 27 cubic feet).

The bag counts are calculated by dividing the total cubic feet (after waste) by the standard yield per bag: 0.60 cubic feet for an 80lb bag, 0.45 for a 60lb bag, and 0.30 for a 40lb bag. Each result is rounded up to the nearest whole bag, since partial bags cannot be purchased or returned unused.

Choosing Between Bags and Ready-Mix

The practical tipping point is around 1 cubic yard (27 cubic feet). Below that volume, bagged concrete is more practical: no minimum order, no truck scheduling, and you can work at your own pace. Above 1 cubic yard, a ready-mix truck becomes cost-competitive and far faster than mixing bags. Ready-mix suppliers typically have a minimum order of 1 yard and charge a short-load fee for smaller quantities, so always get a quote first. For context: a standard 10 ft x 10 ft patio at 4 inches thick is about 1.23 cubic yards, right at the threshold where both options are worth pricing.

Frost Depth and Footing Placement

In climates with freezing winters, footings must extend below the local frost depth to prevent frost heave lifting and cracking the structure above. The frost line ranges from near zero in southern states to more than 5 feet in the northern US and Canada. Your local building department can provide the required depth for your area. When in doubt, dig deeper: the additional concrete cost for a footing that is 6 inches longer is trivial compared to the cost of repairing a heaved post base or deck support.

Cement is an ingredient in concrete - it is not the same thing. Portland cement is a fine gray powder made by heating limestone and clay to extreme temperatures. When mixed with water it forms a paste that binds the other materials together. Concrete is a composite: Portland cement (roughly 10 to 15 percent by volume), fine aggregate such as sand (25 to 30 percent), coarse aggregate such as gravel or crushed stone (40 to 50 percent), and water. Saying you are "pouring cement" when you mean concrete is a very common misnomer - cement alone would be far too brittle and expensive for structural use.
Concrete reaches approximately 70 percent of its 28-day design strength in about 7 days, which is why most building codes use 28 days as the standard cure time for structural purposes. In practical terms: foot traffic is safe after 24 to 48 hours, light vehicle traffic after 7 days, and full load-bearing use after 28 days. Curing is not just drying - it is a chemical hydration reaction that requires moisture. Letting a slab dry out too fast in hot or windy conditions will weaken it. Keep the slab damp by misting, covering with wet burlap, or using a curing compound for at least the first 7 days.
