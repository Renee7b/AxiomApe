Key Culinary and Scaling Terms Explained
Scaling Factor
The multiplier applied to every ingredient. Calculated as Target Servings divided by Original Servings. A factor of 2.5x means each quantity is multiplied by 2.5.
Yield
The total output a recipe produces, expressed in servings, portions, or weight. Doubling the yield means doubling all ingredient quantities.
Imperial vs. Metric
Imperial uses cups, tablespoons, teaspoons, ounces, and pounds. Metric uses grams, kilograms, and milliliters. Grams scale more precisely because they measure weight, not volume.
Baker's Math
A percentage-based scaling system where every ingredient is expressed as a ratio to the total flour weight (flour is always 100%). This makes scaling baked goods to any yield exact and repeatable.
Mirepoix
The classic French flavor base of 2 parts onion, 1 part carrot, 1 part celery by weight. This ratio should be maintained when scaling soups and braises, not just the overall quantities.
Scaling Spices
Spices, salt, and chili heat do not scale linearly. For large batches (3x or more), use 60-75% of the mathematically scaled amount and adjust to taste during cooking.
Mise en Place
French for "everything in its place." The practice of measuring, prepping, and organizing all your scaled ingredients before you begin cooking. Essential for large-batch success.
Culinary Fraction
The traditional way to express cooking measurements: 1/4, 1/3, 1/2, 2/3, 3/4. This tool automatically converts decimal outputs back into these readable fractions so your recipe looks like a real recipe.

Why Recipe Scaling Is Trickier Than Simple Multiplication

For most ingredients, linear scaling works perfectly. 2 cups of flour becomes 4 cups when doubling, 1 cup when halving. But cooking is not always linear, and a few ingredient categories need special attention:

Spices and Salt: When tripling or beyond, add only 60-75% of the calculated spice amount first, then taste and adjust. It is much easier to add salt than to remove it from a finished dish.
Leavening Agents: Baking powder and baking soda can be slightly reduced for large batches. Too much leavening creates a bitter or soapy flavor and can cause cakes to collapse in the center.
Eggs: Eggs come in whole units. If your scaled recipe calls for 2.5 eggs, use 2 and add an extra yolk for richness, or 3 whole eggs if moisture is the priority.
Pan Size: Scaling a recipe does not automatically mean using a bigger pan. If you use the same depth and the same pan type, cooking time stays roughly the same. If you spread the batter across additional pans, each pan may finish a few minutes faster.

Grams vs. Cups: Why Professionals Weigh Everything

A cup of all-purpose flour can weigh anywhere from 120g to 160g depending on whether it was scooped, spooned, or sifted into the measuring cup. That 30% variance is the difference between a dense cookie and a light one. A kitchen scale eliminates this completely. For baking especially, switching from volume measures to weight in grams is the single biggest consistency upgrade a home cook can make. This tool supports grams and kilograms alongside all standard Imperial units for exactly that reason.

Cooking Times When Scaling Up or Down

Oven temperature is determined by the chemistry of the recipe (browning reactions, protein set points, starch gelatinization) and does not change when you scale. Cooking time, however, depends on geometry and thermal mass. A doubled pot of soup takes longer to reach a boil. A cake in the same size pan with the same batter depth takes almost identical time. A scaled recipe spread across two sheet pans instead of one may finish several minutes faster. Always rely on visual doneness cues, an instant-read thermometer, or the toothpick test rather than strict timing when you have changed the batch size significantly.
