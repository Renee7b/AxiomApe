Key Terms Explained
Brew Ratio
The proportion of coffee grounds to water by weight, expressed as 1:X. A 1:15 ratio means 1 gram of coffee for every 15 grams of water.
Dose
The weight of dry coffee grounds used in a single brew, measured in grams. The dose is your primary lever for controlling strength.
Yield
The volume of brewed liquid that actually makes it into your cup after accounting for water absorbed and retained by the spent coffee grounds.
Extraction
The process by which water dissolves soluble compounds (acids, sugars, lipids, caffeine) from coffee grounds. Under-extraction tastes sour; over-extraction tastes bitter.
Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)
A measurement of the concentration of dissolved coffee compounds in your finished brew, expressed as a percentage. Specialty coffee targets roughly 1.15 to 1.45 percent TDS for filter coffee.
Bloom
A pre-infusion pour of hot water (roughly 2 to 3 times the coffee dose) that degasses fresh grounds before the main pour, allowing CO2 to escape and improving even extraction.
Bypass Water
Water added to a finished brew after extraction to dilute concentration without changing extraction percentage. Common in high-concentration brewing methods like Aeropress recipes.
Liquid Retention
The water absorbed by coffee grounds during brewing and lost in the spent puck or filter bed. Ground coffee absorbs approximately 2 times its own weight in water during extraction.

The Complete Guide to Coffee-to-Water Ratios

If your coffee tastes too weak, too bitter, or nothing like what you ordered at your favorite cafe, the brew ratio is almost certainly the first thing to examine. Understanding the ratio between coffee and water - and learning to adjust it deliberately - is the single highest-leverage skill in home brewing.

Why Brew Ratios Matter More Than Grind Size

Grind size controls extraction rate - how fast flavors dissolve. Brew ratio controls extraction concentration - how many dissolved flavors end up per milliliter of water. You can have a perfectly even extraction at any ratio; the difference is how strong or diluted the result tastes. Most brewing problems that seem like grind problems are actually ratio problems. If you increase your dose by just 10 percent and keep everything else constant, you will taste a noticeable increase in body and perceived strength even without touching your grinder.

Standard Ratios by Brew Method

Pour over methods (Hario V60, Chemex, Kalita Wave) perform well at 1:15 to 1:17, with 1:16 as a reliable starting point. French press typically uses 1:12 to 1:15 because immersion brewing extracts more efficiently than drip and a slightly lower ratio prevents over-concentration during the steep. Espresso is radically different: a classic Italian ratio is 1:2 (18 grams in, 36 grams out in roughly 25 to 30 seconds), while modern specialty espresso often uses 1:2.5 to 1:3 for brighter, more transparent flavor. Aeropress recipes vary enormously - the 1:11 preset here reflects a common standard recipe, but concentrated Aeropress recipes at 1:5 with bypass water are equally valid. Cold brew uses 1:8 because the long steep time (12 to 24 hours) extracts more total solids, and the resulting concentrate is typically diluted 1:1 with water or milk before serving.

The Liquid Retention Calculation

Most beginners are surprised to find less liquid in their cup than the water they poured. Coffee grounds act like a sponge: each gram absorbs roughly 2 grams of water during brewing. This tool calculates your estimated liquid yield using the formula: Yield = Total Pour Water - (Coffee Dose x 2). For a 20g dose with 320g of water, that means approximately 40g is absorbed by the grounds, leaving roughly 280g of coffee in your cup. The precise absorption rate varies slightly by grind coarseness, roast level, and freshness, so treat the yield estimate as a close approximation rather than an exact measurement.

Measuring by Weight vs. Volume

A standard tablespoon of coffee weighs approximately 5 to 7 grams depending on grind coarseness and bean density - a range significant enough to completely throw off a recipe. Dark roasts weigh noticeably less per unit volume than light roasts because the roasting process expands the beans and drives off mass as CO2 and moisture. A kitchen scale accurate to 0.1 grams eliminates this variable and costs under $15. Every serious home brewer and professional barista measures both coffee and water by weight.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the golden ratio for brewing coffee? +
The Specialty Coffee Association of America recommends a brewing ratio of 1:18 as a starting benchmark for balanced filter coffee. Most home baristas find the sweet spot falls between 1:15 and 1:17. Pour over brewers typically use 1:15 to 1:16, while French press users often prefer 1:14 to 1:15 for a fuller body. The golden ratio is a starting point, not a rule - your grind size, water temperature, and personal taste should guide the final number.
Does a higher coffee-to-water ratio mean stronger coffee? +
Yes, in simple terms. A higher ratio (for example 1:10 versus 1:17) means more coffee solids dissolved per milliliter of water, which produces a stronger, more concentrated cup. However, strength and extraction quality are different concepts. Increasing your dose without adjusting your grind, water temperature, or brew time can produce a cup that is simultaneously strong and under-extracted (sour or grassy). The goal is a high dose with complete, even extraction.
Why is my final cup of coffee smaller than the amount of water I poured? +
Coffee grounds absorb approximately 2 times their own weight in water during brewing. This absorbed water stays locked in the spent grounds and never makes it into your cup. For example, if you brew with 20 grams of coffee and 300 grams of water, roughly 40 grams (2 x 20g) is absorbed and lost, leaving you with approximately 260 grams of liquid. This liquid retention effect is more noticeable in immersion methods like French press and less pronounced in slow-drip methods where additional pouring can compensate.
How does the brew ratio differ between espresso and French press? +
Espresso uses a very concentrated ratio, typically 1:2 (one gram of coffee to two grams of water by weight). A standard double shot uses 18 to 20 grams of coffee and produces 36 to 40 grams of espresso liquid. French press uses a much more diluted ratio, commonly between 1:12 and 1:15, resulting in a full-bodied but less concentrated beverage. The difference reflects the brewing physics: espresso forces water through tightly packed grounds under high pressure in 25 to 30 seconds, while French press steeps coarsely ground coffee for 4 minutes at atmospheric pressure.
Should I measure coffee by volume (scoops) or by weight (grams)? +
Weight in grams is significantly more accurate and reproducible. Coffee bean density varies widely by roast level - a standard scoop of light roast can weigh up to 20 percent more than a scoop of dark roast because dark roasting expands and lightens the beans. A digital kitchen scale accurate to 0.1 grams removes this variable entirely and lets you dial in your recipe with precision. Most specialty coffee professionals switched to weight-based measurement as soon as affordable scales became available.
