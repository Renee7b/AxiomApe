Key Terms Explained

Fuel Economy Glossary

Definitions for every metric and measurement used by this tool.

MPG (Miles Per Gallon)
How many miles a vehicle can travel on one gallon of fuel. Higher is more efficient. The US and UK each define a gallon differently, so "MPG" alone is ambiguous without specifying which standard.
L/100km (Litres per 100 km)
The volume of fuel (in litres) consumed to travel 100 kilometres. Lower is more efficient. Used by European and many Asian automakers as the official fuel economy standard.
km/L (Kilometres per Litre)
How many kilometres a vehicle travels on one litre of fuel. Higher is more efficient. Common in Japan, India, and parts of Southeast Asia. Analogous to MPG but using metric units.
Imperial Gallon (UK)
The British unit of volume equal to exactly 4.54609 litres (about 277.4 cubic inches). Used for UK MPG ratings. Approximately 20 percent larger than the US liquid gallon.
US Liquid Gallon
The American unit of volume equal to exactly 3.78541 litres (231 cubic inches). Used for US MPG ratings and most North American fuel pump pricing.
Fuel Economy
The ratio of distance a vehicle travels to the amount of fuel it consumes. Often measured under standardised test-cycle conditions by government agencies, meaning real-world figures may differ.
Inverse Metric
When two measurements express the same concept in reciprocal form. MPG and L/100km are inverse metrics: as one rises, the other falls, but not at a constant ratio due to the unit conversion constants involved.
Thermal Efficiency
The percentage of fuel energy that is actually converted to mechanical work. A typical petrol engine achieves 20 to 40 percent thermal efficiency; the rest is lost as heat. Higher thermal efficiency directly improves MPG and L/100km figures.

The Complete Guide to Global Fuel Efficiency Standards

If you have ever rented a car abroad, bought an imported vehicle, or compared specs between an American, British, or European model, you have run into the problem this tool solves: fuel economy figures labelled in one country's units are genuinely confusing when you are used to another. A "50 MPG" rating means something very different on a UK sticker than on a US window label, and neither maps cleanly to the L/100km figure on a German data sheet. This guide explains the standards, the math, and how to use this converter confidently.

Why There Are Four Different Standards

The four formats reflect three separate historical measurement traditions. The United States adopted its own gallon (3.785 L) when it standardised units independently from Britain in the early 1800s. The UK retained its larger Imperial gallon (4.546 L) until metrication began in the 1970s. Continental Europe adopted the metric system far earlier and never used gallons at all, leading to L/100km as the natural default. Japan and India, meanwhile, adopted the metric system but expressed efficiency as km/L (analogous to MPG but metric), which is still the standard in those markets today.

Real-World vs. Official Ratings

Official fuel economy ratings (WLTP in Europe, EPA in the US, JC08/WLTC in Japan) are measured on a test bench under controlled conditions. Real-world efficiency typically runs 10 to 25 percent lower due to driving style, traffic, climate control use, tyre pressure, load, and road gradient. For accurate budget planning, use the consumption figure your vehicle's trip computer reports rather than the window sticker number.

L/100km (litres per 100 kilometres) measures how much fuel a vehicle consumes over a fixed distance, making it easy to directly compare fuel costs. A lower L/100km number is better. km/L inverts this relationship and behaves more like MPG, where a higher number is better. European regulators, automakers, and fuel-economy standards adopted L/100km because it scales linearly with fuel cost: cutting L/100km from 10 to 5 saves exactly twice the fuel over any given trip.
